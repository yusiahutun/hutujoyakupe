Ann Coulter biography

Ann Hart Coulter was born on 8th December 1961 in New York City. Her parents are John Vincent Coulter (father) and Nell Husbands Coulter (mother). Her father was an FBI agent from Albany New York while her mother was born in Paducah, Kentucky.

Ann Coulter education

Ann attended New Canaan High School and graduated in 1980. Later on, she enrolled at Cornell University and played a massive role in The Cornell Review's foundation. She was also a member of the Delta Gamma national sorority. She graduated in 1984 with a Bachelor of Arts Degree in History.

She then enrolled at the University of Michigan Law School. Coulter received her Juris Doctor in 1988. She was an editor of the Michigan Law Review. She was also the president of the local chapter of the Federalist Society.

Editing and writing

Ann kick-started her career immediately after completed her law studies. She served as a law clerk for Pasco Bowman II of the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit in Kansas City. Her area of speciality was corporate law.

After the Republican Party took control of Congress in 1994, she left to work for the United States Senate Judiciary Committee. Some of her duties were:

  • Handling crime and immigration issue for Senator Spencer Abraham of Michigan
  • Helping to craft legislation designed to expedite the deportation of aliens convicted of felonies

Later, Ann became a litigator with the Center for Individuals Rights. In 1996, she was hired as a legal correspondent by MSNBC network even though she was fired shortly after due to her controversial comments. She has also worked for the following:

  • CNN and FOX News
  • Columnist for George Magazine
  • Weekly columnist for Human Events magazines
  • The Roast show

Comments on Islam, Arabs, and terrorism

On 14th September 2001, the journalist commented in her column three days after the 11th September attacks, which resulted in her friend Barbara Olson's death. After making this comment on terrorism, Ann was fired as a columnist by the National Review. She wrote the following:

Airports scrupulously apply the same laughably ineffective airport harassment to Suzy Chapstick as to Muslim hijackers. It is preposterous to assume every passenger is a potential crazed homicidal maniac. We know who the homicidal maniacs are. They are the ones cheering and dancing right now. We should invade their countries, kill their leaders and convert them to Christianity. We weren't punctilious about locating and punishing only Hitler and his top officers. We carpet-bombed German cities; we killed civilians. That's war. And this is war.

In 2002, she criticized the United States Department of Transportation. Her main concern was the department's refusal to use racial profiling as one of airport screening components. This happened after a group of Muslims were barred from a US Airways flight.

Anti-semitism accusations

On 8th October 2007, she was accused of antisemitism during The Big Idea interview with Donny Deutsch. Ann said that the US was a Christian nation, and thus there was the need to perfect Jews, which referred to converting them to Christianity.

In September 2015, she tweeted in response to multiple Republican candidates' references to Israel during a Presidential debate saying:

How many fu*king Jews do these people think there are in the United States?

The Anti-Defamation League referred to the tweet as ugly, spiteful and anti-semitic.

Plagiarism accusations

In October 2001, Ann faced plagiarism accusations because of her 1998 book, High Crimes and Misdemeanors. Michael Chapman, a columnist for the journal Human Events, claimed that passages were taken from a supplement he wrote for the journal in 1997 titled A Case for Impeachment.
