Net-A-Porter now stocks plus-size brand Dima Ayad

 Posted on July 30, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  873 words  |  Aldo Pusey

The conversation around representation and size inclusivity in fashion is nothing new. But what might have started as a murmur, not wanting to disturb fashion’s status quo, has finally found its voice.

The fashion news I’m about to bring you marks a big day – a huge one. It’s not a designer exit from one prestigious house to another, it’s not Jennifer Lopez closing Versace, but to me and the rest of the plus-size community, it’s something even more significant.

For the first time, we're being considered as a collective of consumers that matters. Now, I can click onto global e-commerce site Net-a-Porter and buy a dress. An act that, to some, might seem quite basic – but for me, it's a sign that the industry is really changing.

courtesy

Dima Ayad, a Lebanese designer based in Dubai, was moved to create her brand of bright and bold occasionwear because she wasn’t being served by the fashion industry. Now, her collection of kaftan-style dresses, chiffon gowns and metallic lamé, modelled by Ashley Graham and available in sizes XS to 4XL, will sit along the industry’s most prestigious brands. See I told you: big, huge.

Net-a-Porter has been my go-to destination for every wish list item I’ve ever had. The thrill of unwrapping a new pair of shoes or a new bag is, and always will be, a sartorial high and a huge reward. But that is all I’ve been able to shop across the industry, across every luxury e-commerce platform: accessories. Never once a nice top, or an editor-approved pair of straight-leg jeans, not that of-the-season dress, or an oversized leather jacket. As the global plus-size market currently stands at $60 billion, wouldn’t it be nice if people like me could shop easily for luxury clothes, too?

"I’m finally able to tell women like you that they can shop from us," says Ayad. "And our sizes are a real 2XL and a real 3XL – not fictional sizes that don't fit. We're genuinely size inclusive." But, admits the designer, the journey to get here has not been without its road blocks – and it began with an emotional start.

"In 2010 I was in Mykonos for my best friend’s wedding – it was a time in my life where it felt like every day, there was a wedding – and my luggage didn’t arrive. I am bigger and taller than most, so what was I supposed to do?" recalls Ayad. "I couldn’t just drop into a shop, so I didn’t go to the wedding. And I thought: 'I can’t live like this, it’s time someone did something about this.' There was just nothing for me to wear. Couture was too expensive, so I would go and get fabric and get something made for myself. Then my sister and my mum were asking for pieces, and in 2011 I decided to start my own brand."

With a background in PR and communications, designing wasn’t something Ayad thought she would ever do – but, having taken the leap to create something for herself, she was keen to establish a unique selling point for the brand. "Our USP was to start big and go small. Ninety per cent of the time, if you design big, it will suit someone smaller, but that doesn’t work the other way round."

"When you reward yourself with something that makes you feel good, there is nothing greater"

Fashion has real, transformative power. It has the ability to pick you up when you're down; it’s the armour in which we choose to face the world. Fashion is how we present ourselves to people we both do and don’t know. It’s there for a big moments, and small. "I get a lot of messages like: ‘I met my husband in your dress’; ‘This was the first time I didn’t have to wear Spanx!’; ‘Thank you for making me feel good’. For people who have been overlooked, who haven’t been considered, they feel that I'm thinking about them. We are giving back in the best way possible," explains Ayad. "When you reward yourself with something that makes you feel good and makes you feel seen, there is nothing greater. Women do this every day, but my woman, she doesn’t get to do this every day. She waits for my next collection, for another opportunity to feel seen."

The collection, now available on Net-A-Porter, features metallic pinks, sequins, emerald green and gold, and is totally joyful. On set to shoot the range for its accompanying campaign, it was a sentiment shared. "Ashley Graham was really emotional, she couldn’t believe that the dress just zipped up and fit her perfectly," says Ayad. "There was no need for any alterations, clips or smart styling – it was made for her body.”

Many shoppers won't notice Dima Ayad dropping on this luxury, much-lauded e-tailer's site. However, for the women who have had to hunt for clothes that just might work on their plus-size bodies, this is a celebratory moment – a welcome sign that change is coming.
